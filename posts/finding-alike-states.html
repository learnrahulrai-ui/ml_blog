Title: Finding Alike States (Without the Answers)

We had a sheet of numbers with 50 states and their crime counts (Murder, Assault, UrbanPop). We wanted to know which states were the most alike, but we had no answers to copy. Here is how we solved it using just pencil and paper.

## 1. Humbling the Numbers (The Fair Ruler)
If we just looked at the raw numbers, Assault was massive (going up to 335) and Murder was tiny (only up to 17.4). Assault would boss the other columns around. We had to fix the ruler.

We took each column and found two things:
1. The middle (the average)
2. The spread (we took the gaps from the middle, squared them, found the average of the squares, and took the square root)

Then we shrunk every single number in the sheet using this rule: `(number - middle) / spread`.

This gave us a new, humbled sheet. We proved that for every humbled column, the new middle is exactly 0, and the new spread is exactly 1. Now they are perfectly fair.

## 2. Measuring the Gap
To see how alike two states are, we measured the straight-line gap between them. 
Since we had 3 columns, we couldn't use flat graph paper. We put our agents in an empty room, where Murder and Assault were the floor, and UrbanPop was the ceiling.

For a pair of states like Alabama and Alaska, the agent:
1. Squared the Murder gap
2. Squared the Assault gap
3. Squared the UrbanPop gap
4. Added all three squares together, and took the square root.

That one final number (the length of the straight line through the empty room) was handed back to us.

## 3. The Giant Sheet of Gaps
We did that same math for every single pair of states. We wrote all the lengths down in a giant 50x50 square table. We called this the "sheet of gaps" (the Distance Matrix).

## 4. The Closest Neighbors
With our giant sheet filled out, finding the most alike states was easy. 
Since a big number means the states are totally different, we just hunted the table for the absolute smallest number (the minimum gap). The two states that owned that box were our perfect twins.

If we wanted the closest neighbors for California, we just looked at California's row, found the smallest numbers, and looked up to the top edge to read off their names. 

And that is how we sorted out the pile of numbers without any answers.
