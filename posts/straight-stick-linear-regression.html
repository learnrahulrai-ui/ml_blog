The Straight Stick: Linear Regression from Three Points
Posted: 2026-06-01  |  Author: Rahul Rai  |  Tags: linear-regression, ols, least-squares, by-hand, math

The neighbour machine copies. This one is a completely different philosophy. No neighbours, no scanning the pile. Instead:

Give every measured thing a dial. Multiply and add up.
guess = rooms×dial&sub1; + income×dial&sub2; + age×dial&sub3; + … + nudge

Each dial is a multiplier that says how much that measurement matters. The nudge is the baseline — the guess when every measurement is zero. Once the dials are set, guessing is instant: no memory, just arithmetic.

The Bowl

The best dials are the ones that make the total mistake smallest. Picture a bowl — the height is the mistake, and you want the bottom: 

         ╲         ╱
          ╲       ╱
           ╲     ╱
            ╲   ╱
             ╲ ╱
              •  ← best dials live here (bottom of the bowl)

Two ways to find the bottom:

  1. Walk downhill — start anywhere, feel the slope, take a small step down, repeat. (The textbook calls this gradient descent.)
  2. Solve directly — the slope is zero at the bottom; for a straight stick you can solve where flat in one shot.

For a straight line there's an exact answer, so I wanted the real math. Here it is — three points, done entirely by pencil.

The Pencil Solve

   x = 1, 2, 3       y = 2, 3, 5

   Build the sums:
   Σ1  = 3    Σx  = 6     Σy  = 10    Σxy = 23    Σx² = 14

   Normal equations (the "where flat" conditions):
     3b +  6w = 10
     6b + 14w = 23

   Solve:
     multiply top row by 2:   6b + 12w = 20
     subtract from bottom:          2w = 3     →  w = 3/2
     back-substitute:    3b + 9 = 10           →  b = 1/3

   The line:  ŷ = 1/3 + 1.5x

Now check the residuals — the leftover misses:

   x=1: ŷ=1.833, miss=+0.167
   x=2: ŷ=3.333, miss=−0.333
   x=3: ŷ=4.833, miss=+0.167

   Sum of misses   = 0     ✓  (they cancel)
   Sum of x·miss   = 0     ✓  (residuals ⟂ inputs)
   RMSE ≈ 0.236
★ THE SAME OPERATION, SCALED This three-point pencil solve is exactly what the machine does — just with 16,000 rows and 8 dials instead of 3 rows and 1 dial. The dials live in m.coef_, the nudge in m.intercept_.

The Geometry: It's a Projection

Those two checks aren't a coincidence. The residuals sum to zero and they're perpendicular to the inputs because fitting a straight line is a projection. The best line is the shadow of the data cast onto the space of all possible lines. You can't do better than the shadow — any other line leaves a longer leftover.

And here it is in symbols, for when you're ready to go deeper:

   minimise  J(β) = ‖Xβ − y‖²          (total squared miss)
   set slope to zero:  XᵀX β = Xᵀy      (the normal equations)
   solve:  β = (XᵀX)⁻¹ Xᵀy             (the exact dials)

   geometry:  ŷ = Hy   →  it's a projection (H = "hat" matrix)
   residual ⟂ inputs:   Xᵀe = 0
   stats:  unbiased, best among all straight guessers (Gauss–Markov)

I won't pretend I absorbed all of that in one sitting. But I touched it — I held the pieces. That matters more than memorising the final formula.

In Code

The tool hides the loop again. fit does the real work (solving the normal equations); predict is just multiply-and-add: 

from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression
from sklearn.metrics import mean_squared_error

m = LinearRegression()           # the straight-stick machine, no neighbour knob
m.fit(X_train, y_train)          # finds the dials (the real work)
guesses = m.predict(X_test)

rmse = mean_squared_error(y_test, guesses) ** 0.5
print(round(rmse, 3))

print("dials (coef_):", m.coef_)
print("nudge (intercept_):", m.intercept_)
▸ No knob to tune Unlike KNN's k, plain linear regression has nothing to choose. The dials are solved, not picked. There's no equivalent of "try k=1, 3, 5…" — the bottom of the bowl is wherever the math says it is. (That changes once you add regularisation — a later chapter.)

KNN vs. The Straight Stick

 Neighbour machine (KNN)Straight stick (linear)

"Training"none — just memorisesolve for the dials once
Guessing costre-scan whole pileinstant multiply-add
Knob to pickk (cross-validate it)none (it's solved)
Shape it fitsbumpy, localone flat plane

Same job, opposite strategies. One leans on memory, the other on a rule. Both answer the same question: given what I've seen, what's my best guess for something I haven't?

Where to Go Deeper

When you're ready, these are the search terms that open up the math under the straight stick:

  • dot product, matrix times vector — the language
  • normal equations, least squares — the solve
  • least squares projection — the geometry
  • gradient descent — the walk-downhill alternative
  • Gauss–Markov — why this line is provably the best straight guesser
  1. A straight rule = a dial per feature, plus one nudge.
  2. Best dials = the bottom of the squared-mistake bowl.
  3. For a line you can solve the bottom exactly (normal equations).
  4. Residuals sum to zero and are perpendicular to the inputs — it's a projection.
  5. Plain linear regression has no knob to tune; the dials are solved.
