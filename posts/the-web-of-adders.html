  Every machine in this book so far had ONE room of clerks: thirty measurements came in,
  thirty dials multiplied them, one number (or one chance) came out. That single room drew
  straight lines and smooth curves -- useful, but limited. A tumour is not a straight line.

  This chapter stacks rooms. Thirty measurements enter Room 1, sixteen clerks multiply and
  add, and sixteen numbers leave. Those sixteen enter Room 2. Eight clerks process them.
  Eight numbers leave. One final lonely clerk turns those eight into a chance. THAT is the
  building full of clerks -- what the world calls a neural network.

  By the end of this post you will have walked one student through the building by hand,
  step by arithmetic step, and arrived at a final guess and a loss. No surprises, no magic --
  just 496 dials doing their arithmetic, one room at a time.


  ## The Sheet and the Three Cuts

  The sheet: 569 patients. Thirty measurements each (radius, texture, smoothness, ...).
  One mark column: M (malignant) or B (benign). The goal: a building that guesses the mark.

  Every chapter so far cut the pile in TWO: a study pile and a sealed exam. That was enough
  when the only knob to pick was one number (k, or lambda, or the tree depth). The building
  full of clerks has many more knobs: how many rooms, how many clerks per room, how many
  passes of study, how aggressively to fire lazy clerks. Picking those knobs while peeking
  at the sealed exam would be cheating -- so we need a THIRD pile, a practice pile, to grade
  freely during tuning, while the sealed exam waits untouched.

  The three-cut recipe (plain numbers, no trust):

    Start: 100 students total (we will use 100 for simplicity; same arithmetic on 569).
    Cut 1: seal 20 in an envelope -- the EXAM pile. Never touch it until the final grade.
            100 - 20 = 80 remain.
    Cut 2: hide 20 more for watching the machine while it learns -- the PRACTICE pile.
            80 - 20 = 60 remain.
    What is left: 60 students -- the STUDY pile.

    Check: 60 + 20 + 20 = 100. The three piles tile the original perfectly.

    Why two cuts, not one? Cut 1 keeps the final grade honest. Cut 2 lets us pick knobs
    without spending the honest grade. If we used the exam pile to pick the number of rooms,
    the exam is no longer a surprise -- the building was shaped around it.

  A picture of the two cuts:

                    [ 100 students ]
                       /          \
                      /            \
              [ 80 open ]      [ 20 exam (sealed) ]
                 /    \
                /      \
       [ 60 study ]  [ 20 practice ]

  CHOICE: these specific fractions (60/20/20). Other splits (70/15/15, 80/10/10) are also
  reasonable. The exact fractions are a modelling choice, not a mathematical theorem.

  >> YOUR TURN
     We have 455 patients. We want a 60/20/20 split. How many go in each pile?

     check your slate:
       Exam pile:     455 x 0.20 = 91 patients sealed.
       Open:          455 - 91 = 364 remain.
       Practice pile: 364 x 0.25 = 91 patients hidden.
                      (25% of the open 80% = 20% of the full total)
       Study pile:    364 - 91 = 273.
       Check: 273 + 91 + 91 = 455. ✓


  ## Humbling the Numbers

  IN HAND: 569 patients, 30 measurements, cut into 60/20/20. This section prepares the
  measurements for the clerks: column A (radius) runs 10 to 28; column B (smoothness)
  runs 0.05 to 0.16. If we feed these raw, the clerks multiply the radius dial by numbers
  ~15 and the smoothness dial by numbers ~0.1. The radius dial does 150x the work. The
  smoothness dial might as well be zero. The building learns about radius; smoothness vanishes.

  THE FIX -- put every column on the same ruler:

    humbled value = (raw value - study mean) / study spread

  where study mean = average of that column across the 60 study patients only, and
  study spread = standard deviation of that column across the 60 study patients only.

  CHOICE: use standard deviation as the spread measure.

  After humbling, every column has mean 0 and spread 1. The radius dial and smoothness
  dial now multiply numbers of similar size. Neither swamps the other.

  THE LEAK TRAP: why compute from the STUDY pile only?

    The exam pile is sealed because it represents patients the building has never seen.
    If we compute the mean using all 569 patients (study + practice + exam combined),
    the exam pile's own measurements drift into the mean. The mean is then calibrated
    to the exam pile's values. The ruler applied to the exam patients is no longer
    blind -- it was shaped by those very patients. That leaks information from the
    exam into the study. The sealed envelope is no longer sealed.

    In numbers: suppose exam patient #7 is an extreme outlier -- radius = 40.
    Include them in the mean: study mean rises. Every study patient's humbled radius
    shifts down slightly. The building trains on numbers shifted toward patient #7's
    value. When it later sees patient #7 on the exam, it was already leaning that way.
    Exclude patient #7 from the mean: the mean is purely the study pile's own centre;
    the building trains on numbers that have nothing to do with the exam.

  >> YOUR TURN
     The study pile has mean 15.0 and spread 2.0 for the radius column.
     A practice-pile patient has raw radius 17.0. What is their humbled score?

     check your slate:
       humbled = (17.0 - 15.0) / 2.0 = 2.0 / 2.0 = 1.0.
       This patient is 1.0 spreads above the study average -- mildly above average. ✓

  !! WARN: THE RULER MUST NOT BE RE-LEARNED ON THE PRACTICE OR EXAM PILE
     Compute the mean and spread ONCE from the study pile. Apply that same ruler to all
     three piles. If you re-learn the mean on the practice pile, each pile lives on a
     different ruler -- the building learns one scale, grades on another.


  ## The Machine's Shape

  IN HAND: 569 patients, 30 measurements humbled from the study pile mean and spread.
  This section builds the building: how many rooms, how many clerks, how the dials connect.

  The straight-stick machine had ONE room: 30 dials, one per measurement, one fixed nudge,
  one number out. Fine for straight lines. A tumour boundary is not straight. Stacking
  rooms of clerks lets the building draw jagged, curved, arbitrarily complex boundaries.

  Our building (CHOICE: 3 rooms, sizes 16 / 8 / 1):

    Front door: 30 measurements per patient
      |
      v
    ROOM 1: 16 clerks
      Each clerk connects to ALL 30 measurements.
      Each clerk has 30 dials (one per measurement) + 1 fixed nudge.
      Each clerk outputs 1 number.
      Total out of Room 1: 16 numbers.
      |
      v
    [ZERO-OUT SQUASHER -- explained in next section]
      |
      v
    ROOM 2: 8 clerks
      Each clerk connects to all 16 numbers from Room 1.
      Each clerk has 16 dials + 1 fixed nudge.
      Total out of Room 2: 8 numbers.
      |
      v
    [ZERO-OUT SQUASHER]
      |
      v
    ROOM 3: 1 clerk
      Connects to all 8 numbers from Room 2.
      1 dial per input + 1 nudge.
      Total out: 1 raw score.
      |
      v
    [S-CURVE SQUASHER -- explained after]
      |
      v
    FINAL GUESS: a chance between 0 and 1.

  Counting the dials and nudges in Room 1:

    Each of the 16 clerks has 30 dials:   16 x 30 = 480 dials
    Each clerk also has 1 nudge:          16 nudges
    Room 1 total:                         480 + 16 = 496 numbers to learn

  These 480 dials are arranged in a grid: 30 rows (one per measurement) by 16 columns
  (one per clerk). This grid is called the WEIGHT MATRIX. One clerk's arithmetic:

    Clerk j's raw score =
      (x1 x dial_1j) + (x2 x dial_2j) + ... + (x30 x dial_30j) + nudge_j

  Clerk-step cost for Room 1, one patient:
    30 multiplications per clerk x 16 clerks = 480 multiplications
    29 additions to sum 30 products,  x 16   = 464 additions
    16 nudge additions                        = 16  additions
    Total per patient:                        = 960 arithmetic operations

  For 341 study patients per pass: 341 x 960 = 327,360 operations -- done by lunch
  for a room of tireless clerks; unthinkable for one pencil alone.

  >> YOUR TURN
     Room 2 receives 16 numbers from Room 1 and has 8 clerks.
     Count the dials and nudges in Room 2.

     check your slate:
       8 clerks x 16 inputs each = 128 dials.
       8 nudges.
       Room 2 total: 128 + 8 = 136 parameters. ✓


  ## Why Stacking Without the Bend Collapses

  IN HAND: 30 measurements, Room 1 (16 clerks) feeding Room 2 (8 clerks). We have NOT
  yet added any special rule between the rooms. This section shows why the rooms collapse
  into a single room without one -- and states the fix.

  THEOREM (the linear collapse):

    Suppose every clerk just multiplies by dials and adds nudges, then passes the result
    directly to the next room with no interruption.

    Clerk A in Room 1 computes: zA = (x1 x w1) + (x2 x w2)
    Clerk B in Room 2 takes zA and multiplies by its own dial v:

      zB = zA x v = ( (x1 x w1) + (x2 x w2) ) x v
                  = (x1 x w1 x v) + (x2 x w2 x v)

    That final expression is just: x1 times a new dial (w1 x v), plus x2 times another
    new dial (w2 x v). That is the STRAIGHT-STICK MACHINE with combined dials.

    Adding a third, fourth, or hundredth room -- same argument. At every step, the
    combined dials are just products of the previous dials. No matter how deep the
    building, it is arithmetically identical to a single room.

    Proof in one line: call the combined transform through N plain rooms L_N.
    L_1 = W_1. L_{N+1} = W_{N+1} x L_N. The product of matrices is a matrix --
    one room. QED.

  The fix: interrupt the flow BETWEEN rooms with a rule that CANNOT be absorbed into
  a dial multiplication. Then the combined-dial argument breaks.


  ## The Zero-Out Squasher (ReLU)

  The interrupting rule:

    If a clerk's score is less than 0, replace it with 0.
    If a clerk's score is 0 or above, keep it as-is.
    In one symbol: A = max(0, Z)

  Three examples:

    Z = +2.4  ->  max(0, 2.4) = 2.4   (positive, unchanged)
    Z = -0.7  ->  max(0, -0.7) = 0    (negative, zeroed)
    Z =  0.0  ->  max(0, 0.0) = 0     (the boundary, zeroed / kept, same result)

  THEOREM (the bend cannot be absorbed):

    Multiplication by any constant C maps 0 to 0: 0 x C = 0.
    The max rule does NOT: max(0, -3) = 0, but if we had kept -3 and multiplied,
    -3 x 2 = -6 ≠ 0. The rule changes the value in a way no dial multiplication can
    undo. The combined-dial collapse argument from above requires that every step is
    a multiplication -- once max(0, Z) is applied, that argument cannot proceed.

    In a picture: the function y = max(0, x) has a SHARP CORNER at x=0 (flat line
    for x<0, diagonal line for x>=0). No multiplication -- which just rescales a
    straight line -- can produce a sharp corner. Therefore the zero-out squasher is
    not a multiplication, and the collapse is broken. QED.

  CHOICE: this specific squasher (zero below, unchanged above) rather than alternatives
  (smooth S-curve at every room, which is more expensive; leaky versions that let a
  small signal through below zero). Any non-linear rule that breaks the collapse will
  do; this one is the cheapest to compute.

  Clerk-step cost: 1 comparison per score. Room 1, 341 patients, 16 clerks:
    341 x 16 = 5,456 comparisons. Tiny next to the 327,360 from the multiplications.

  !! WARN: THE DEAD CLERK
     If every input to a clerk is hugely negative, the clerk's raw score is always
     negative, the zero-out rule always outputs 0, and the next room never hears from
     it. That clerk is "dead." If ALL 16 clerks in Room 1 die at once, Room 2 and 3
     receive only zeros and the building cannot learn anything. This happens when the
     initial dials are set too large in the wrong direction. Most frameworks start with
     small random dials that prevent this. If you set all dials to zero by hand, you
     will see it.

  >> YOUR TURN
     Three scores emerge from Room 1: Z = [2.4, -0.7, 0.0].
     Apply the zero-out squasher to each.

     check your slate:
       2.4  >= 0  ->  keep  2.4
       -0.7 <  0  ->  write 0
       0.0  >= 0  ->  keep  0   (the boundary counts as non-negative)
       A = [2.4, 0, 0]. ✓


  ## The S-Curve at the Exit

  IN HAND: 30 measurements humbled, three-cut split, Room 1 (16 clerks, zero-out after),
  Room 2 (8 clerks, zero-out after), Room 3 (1 clerk, no zero-out). Room 3 produces one
  raw score -- any real number. This section adds the final step: bend that score into
  a chance between 0 and 1.

  The S-curve squasher was derived from ODDS in post 6 (Sorting Into Bins). Re-state the
  result (IOU: full derivation paid in post 6):

    S(Z) = 1 / (1 + e^(-Z))

  where e is approximately 2.718 (the natural base).

  Three key values, recomputed here:

    Z = 0:
      S(0) = 1 / (1 + e^0) = 1 / (1 + 1) = 1/2 = 0.5   (50% -- exactly on the fence)

    Z = +4:
      e^(-4) = 1 / e^4.  e^4 = e x e x e x e = 2.718 x 2.718 x 2.718 x 2.718.
      e^2 = 2.718 x 2.718 = 7.39.  e^4 = 7.39 x 7.39 = 54.6.
      e^(-4) = 1/54.6 = 0.018.
      S(4)  = 1 / (1 + 0.018) = 1 / 1.018 = 0.982       (98.2% -- nearly certain sick)

    Z = -4:
      e^(-(-4)) = e^4 = 54.6 (from above).
      S(-4) = 1 / (1 + 54.6) = 1 / 55.6 = 0.018         (1.8% -- nearly certain well)

  CHOICE: use S-curve at the exit for a yes/no question. For "guess a number" (regression)
  the exit would produce the raw score directly with no squasher. Other exit choices exist
  for three-or-more bins (Softmax -- a later post).


  ## The Forward Walk by Hand

  IN HAND: 1 student, measurements x = [0.5, -1.2] (already humbled), true answer = 1
  (malignant). A tiny building: 2 clerks in Room 1, 1 clerk in Room 2. This section walks
  that student through the building, one arithmetic step per line.

  CHOICE: toy building of 2 inputs and 2+1 clerks to keep the arithmetic on one page.
  A real building has 30 inputs and 16+8+1 clerks. The arithmetic is identical -- just
  more rows of the same steps.

  --- ROOM 1: CLERK 1 ---

    Dials: [2.0, 1.0].  Fixed nudge: 0.1.

    Step 1: measurement 1 x dial 1:   0.5  x  2.0  =   1.0
    Step 2: measurement 2 x dial 2:  -1.2  x  1.0  =  -1.2
    Step 3: add the products:          1.0  + (-1.2) =  -0.2
    Step 4: add the nudge:            -0.2  +  0.1   =  -0.1

    Clerk 1 raw score: Z1_1 = -0.1

  --- ROOM 1: CLERK 2 ---

    Dials: [-1.0, 0.5].  Fixed nudge: -0.2.

    Step 1: measurement 1 x dial 1:   0.5  x (-1.0) =  -0.5
    Step 2: measurement 2 x dial 2:  -1.2  x  0.5   =  -0.6
    Step 3: add the products:         -0.5  + (-0.6) =  -1.1
    Step 4: add the nudge:            -1.1  + (-0.2) =  -1.3

    Clerk 2 raw score: Z1_2 = -1.3

  Raw paper leaving Room 1: Z1 = [-0.1, -1.3]

  --- THE ZERO-OUT SQUASHER ---

    Apply max(0, Z) to each score:
      -0.1 < 0  ->  write 0
      -1.3 < 0  ->  write 0

    Squashed paper entering Room 2: A1 = [0, 0]

    Both clerks were negative; both outputs are zero. This is the dead-clerk case.
    Room 2 receives all zeros. The building still computes (the nudge in Room 2 carries
    a signal), but this student was entirely wiped by Room 1. The dials started badly.

  --- ROOM 2: THE FINAL LONELY CLERK ---

    Dials: [3.0, -2.0].  Fixed nudge: 0.5.

    Step 1: A1[0] x dial 1:  0  x  3.0   = 0
    Step 2: A1[1] x dial 2:  0  x (-2.0) = 0
    Step 3: add the products: 0  +  0     = 0
    Step 4: add the nudge:    0  +  0.5   = 0.5

    Final raw score: Z2 = 0.5

  --- THE S-CURVE EXIT ---

    A2 = S(Z2) = 1 / (1 + e^(-0.5))

    Computing e^(-0.5):
      e^0.5 is the square root of e.  e = 2.718.
      sqrt(2.718): 1.6 x 1.6 = 2.56 (too low).  1.65 x 1.65 = 2.7225 (close).
      e^0.5 = 1.649 (use 1.649).
      e^(-0.5) = 1 / 1.649 = 0.607.

    1 + 0.607 = 1.607.
    1 / 1.607: try 0.62 x 1.607 = 0.997 (close).  Final A2 = 0.622.

    The building says: 62.2% chance malignant.

    True answer: 1 (malignant). The building is leaning the right way, weakly.
    After the dials are adjusted by studying many patients, the guess will sharpen.

  >> YOUR TURN
     Same building. New student: x = [1, 2, 3], entering Room 1 with 2 clerks.
       Clerk 1: dials [0.1, 0.2, 0.1], nudge 0.5.
       Clerk 2: dials [-0.1, 0.0, 0.2], nudge 0.0.

     check your slate:
       Step 1: 1 x (-0.1)  = -0.1

       Step 3: 3 x 0.2     =  0.6
       Sum: -0.1 + 0.0 + 0.6 = 0.5
       Step 4: add nudge: 0.5 + 0.0 = 0.5
       Clerk 2 raw score = 0.5. ✓


  ## Grading the Guess (Cross-Entropy Loss)

  IN HAND: 1 student, x = [0.5, -1.2], true answer 1, building guess A2 = 0.622.
  This section measures how wrong the guess is.

  Post 6 (Sorting Into Bins) derived the loss formula from first principles (IOU paid
  there). The result, restated:

    when the true answer is 1:   loss = -ln(guess)
    when the true answer is 0:   loss = -ln(1 - guess)

  For this student (true = 1, guess = 0.622):

    loss = -ln(0.622)

  Computing ln(0.622):
    ln is the natural logarithm (base e = 2.718).
    Known anchor: ln(0.5) = -0.693.   (since e^(-0.693) = 0.5)
    Known anchor: ln(0.6) = -0.511.
    0.622 is between 0.6 and 0.625, closer to 0.625.
    ln(0.625) ≈ -0.470.
    ln(0.622) ≈ -0.475 (interpolating).
    loss = -(-0.475) = 0.475.

  This is the dot plotted on the "wrongness vs pass number" chart.  As the dials improve,
  this number falls toward zero.

  How bad is 0.475? Two comparison cases:

    Perfect-ish guess (A2 = 0.99, true = 1):
      loss = -ln(0.99).  ln(0.99) = ln(1 - 0.01) ≈ -0.01 (for small x, ln(1-x) ≈ -x).
      loss ≈ 0.010.

    Clueless guess (A2 = 0.50, true = 1):
      loss = -ln(0.50) = 0.693 (from the anchor above, flipped).

    Our guess of 0.622 gives loss 0.475 -- between the clueless (0.693) and the sharp
    (0.010). The dials have something to learn but are already slightly right.

  >> YOUR TURN
     A patient with true answer 0 (benign). The building guesses 0.30.
     Compute the loss. (True = 0, so use loss = -ln(1 - guess).)

     check your slate:
       loss = -ln(1 - 0.30) = -ln(0.70).
       ln(0.7): ln(0.5) = -0.693; ln(1.0) = 0.  Midway: ln(0.7) ≈ -0.357.
       loss ≈ 0.357.
       The building guessed 30% malignant on a well patient.  Moderate wrongness. ✓


  ## Rolling Downhill (Backpropagation and Adam)

  Room 1 has 480 dials + 16 nudges = 496. Room 2 has 16 dials + 8 nudges = 24.
  Room 3 has 8 dials + 1 nudge = 9. Total building: 496 + 24 + 9 = 529 dials and nudges.
  (Recomputed here from scratch: 480+16 + 16+8 + 8+1 = 529. ✓)

  To adjust 529 dials by testing each one larger and smaller -- one at a time -- costs:

    2 x 529 tests x 341 patients per test = 360,778 full forward passes per tiny adjustment

  That is done by Christmas, not lunch. The smarter way: compute the SLOPE of the loss
  with respect to every dial simultaneously, by running the error BACKWARDS through the
  rooms. This is BACKPROPAGATION (sending the error backward, room by room, through the
  calculus of slopes -- the chain rule of derivatives).

  IOU: the full derivation of backpropagation requires calculus. That debt is open --
  it will be paid in a later post. Here we use the result: backpropagation delivers
  one slope number per dial in roughly 2x the cost of one forward pass.

  Clerk-step cost for one forward-plus-backward pass on 341 patients:
    Forward through all three rooms: roughly 341 x (960 + something smaller) ~ 341,000 ops.
    Backward: roughly the same again.
    Total per pass: roughly 2 x 341,000 = 682,000 operations -- still done by lunch.

  CHOICE (the LEARNING RATE): how far each dial turns per pass.
    Too large -> dial overshoots the bottom of the wrongness hill and spirals away.
    Too small -> dial creeps; may still be crawling by Christmas.

  CHOICE (ADAM, the dynamic step manager): rather than a fixed step size, Adam watches
  the slope history of each dial and takes large steps when the slope is steep, tiny steps
  when it flattens. This prevents both overshooting and creeping.

    STUBBORN (step too small): creeps toward the bottom, never arrives.
    PANICKY (step too big): leaps past the bottom, spirals outward.
    ADAM: reads the slope, sizes the step, arrives at a decent bottom in a reasonable time.


  ## Lazy Clerks and the Coffee Break (Dropout)

  Let the building study for 50 full passes. After a few passes, Clerk 1 in Room 1 happens
  to be the sharpest -- its dials happened to start well. The other 15 clerks notice:
  they can stop adjusting their own dials and simply copy Clerk 1's output. The study loss
  still falls. But the building has now memorised the study pile's specific quirks, not the
  general pattern. Hand it the practice pile: the quirks do not match. Practice loss climbs
  while study loss falls. THAT is overfitting.

  The fix is DROPOUT: before each pass of study, pick 20% of clerks at random and send
  them on a coffee break. Their output is forced to 0 for that pass.

  Room 1, 16 clerks, 20% dropout:  16 x 0.20 = 3.2 -> 3 clerks sent on break per pass.
  (In practice, the exact number varies pass to pass; 20% is the average.)

  Because Clerk 1 might be sent on break at any moment, the lazy clerks cannot rely on
  Clerk 1. Each clerk must maintain its own dials. The building is forced to distribute
  the work across all 16 clerks rather than piling it onto one.

  CHOICE: the dropout fraction (0.20 here; 0.10 to 0.50 are all used in practice).

  CHOICE: dropout only during STUDY, not during grading. During the practice-pile grade
  and the sealed-exam grade, all clerks report for duty. This is done automatically by
  any standard framework.

  >> YOUR TURN
     Room 1 has 16 clerks. Dropout rate = 0.25.
     How many clerks take a coffee break per pass, on average?

     check your slate:
       16 x 0.25 = 4 clerks sent on break.  12 remain working. ✓


  ## The Gear Shatter Trap (32-bit Overflow)

  The clerks use 32-bit arithmetic. A 32-bit number can hold up to roughly e^88 before
  it breaks (IEEE 754 single precision).

  The S-curve squasher needs e^(-Z). If Z = -500:
    e^(-(-500)) = e^500.
    e^88 is the largest safe value.  500 >> 88.
    The gear shatters. The machine prints: RuntimeWarning: overflow encountered in exp.
    The output is inf or nan, not a probability. Every subsequent calculation inherits nan.

  THE FIX: before computing the S-curve, clip Z to the range [-80, +80].

    If Z <  -80, replace Z with -80.
    If Z >  +80, replace Z with +80.
    Otherwise, keep Z unchanged.

  Effect of the clip on accuracy:
    S(-80) = 1 / (1 + e^80).
    e^80 is about 5.5 x 10^34 (still within 32-bit range because 80 < 88).
    1 / (1 + 5.5 x 10^34) is essentially 0 -- indistinguishable from exactly 0 at
    any level of medical decision-making.  The clip changes the output by less than
    one part in 10^34.  The gear shatter is avoided at essentially zero cost to accuracy.

  Clerk-step cost: 1 comparison per S-curve call. Negligible.

  >> YOUR TURN
     Z = -120. We clip to -80. What does the S-curve output, approximately?
     Is this patient likely malignant or benign?

     check your slate:
       After clip: Z = -80.
       e^(-(-80)) = e^80 ≈ 5.5 x 10^34.
       S(-80) = 1 / (1 + 5.5 x 10^34) ≈ 1.8 x 10^(-35) ≈ 0.
       The building outputs essentially 0% malignant.
       Verdict: almost certain benign. ✓


  ## Common Tripwires

  From real lab struggles with this building -- not invented.

  !! WARN: THE LEAK TRAP
     "I called scaler.fit(X) on all 569 rows before splitting."
     The exam pile's values bleed into the mean and spread. The building is secretly
     calibrated to the exam pile. The final grade is a lie.
     FIX: fit the scaler on X_train ONLY, then transform all three piles with that scaler.

  !! WARN: FORGETTING THE S-CURVE AT THE EXIT
     "My final guess was 14.7. I thresholded at 0.5 and it worked."
     14.7 > 0.5 happens to give the right label on some patients, but the loss formula
     takes ln(14.7), which is positive, so the formula returns a negative loss -- a number
     that means nothing. Negative wrongness is not possible.
     FIX: always apply S-curve to the final clerk's score before thresholding or computing loss.

  !! WARN: MIXING THE PILES
     "I got 100% accuracy!"
     You graded the building on the study pile -- the pile it memorised.
     FIX: grade on the practice or sealed exam pile, never on the study pile.

  !! WARN: DIMENSION MISMATCH
     "ValueError: shapes (341, 30) and (16, 30) not aligned"
     The weight matrix for Room 1 must be (30, 16): 30 rows, 16 columns, so that
     (341, 30) @ (30, 16) = (341, 16). If the matrix is stored (16, 30) -- transposed --
     the shapes don't align and the machine crashes.
     FIX: the matrix multiply is X @ W, not W @ X. Check: X is (patients, measurements);
     W must be (measurements, clerks). The output is (patients, clerks).

  !! WARN: ZERO-OUT SQUASHER AT THE EXIT
     "I used ReLU after Room 3 and fed it into the loss formula."
     The cross-entropy loss expects a number strictly between 0 and 1. ReLU at the exit
     can produce any non-negative number. ln(14.7) is positive; the loss formula breaks.
     FIX: S-curve (Sigmoid) at the exit for yes/no. ReLU in the middle rooms only.

  !! WARN: DROPOUT DURING GRADING
     "My practice accuracy is wildly different every time I run model.evaluate()."
     Dropout was left on during grading. Random clerks were silenced on each call,
     giving a different answer each time.
     FIX: in Keras, model.evaluate() and model.predict() disable dropout automatically.
     Only model.fit() uses it. Never use Dropout during a manual grading loop.

  section is for the day you meet one: the same steps, spoken in Python and Keras.

  Five blocks, in order: split, scale, build, study, grade.

    import numpy as np
    import pandas as pd
    from sklearn.model_selection import train_test_split
    from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
    import tensorflow as tf
    from tensorflow.keras.models import Sequential
    from tensorflow.keras.layers import Dense, Dropout

    RANDOM_STATE = 42

    # --- The three cuts ---
    X_temp, X_test,  y_temp, y_test  = train_test_split(
        X, y, test_size=0.20, random_state=RANDOM_STATE
    )
    X_train, X_val,  y_train, y_val  = train_test_split(
        X_temp, y_temp, test_size=0.25, random_state=RANDOM_STATE
        # 25% of the open 80% = 20% of the full total
    )

    # --- Humbling the numbers (fit on study pile only) ---
    scaler = StandardScaler()
    X_train_scaled = scaler.fit_transform(X_train)   # compute mean/spread from study + apply
    X_val_scaled   = scaler.transform(X_val)          # apply the STUDY ruler -- do NOT refit
    X_test_scaled  = scaler.transform(X_test)         # apply the STUDY ruler -- exam never peeked

    # --- The building ---
    model = Sequential([
        Dense(16, activation='relu', input_shape=(X_train.shape[1],)),
        Dropout(0.20),                         # send 20% of Room 1 clerks on coffee break
        Dense(8,  activation='relu'),
        Dropout(0.20),
        Dense(1,  activation='sigmoid'),       # S-curve exit: output is a chance 0 to 1
    ])

    # --- Hire Adam, measure wrongness by cross-entropy ---
    model.compile(
        optimizer='adam',
        loss='binary_crossentropy',
        metrics=['accuracy']
    )

    # --- Study for 50 passes, watching the practice pile ---
    history = model.fit(
        X_train_scaled, y_train,
        epochs=50,
        batch_size=32,             # hand the clerks 32 students at a time before adjusting
        validation_data=(X_val_scaled, y_val),
        verbose=1
    )

    # --- Grade on the sealed exam (once, at the very end) ---
    exam_loss, exam_accuracy = model.evaluate(X_test_scaled, y_test, verbose=0)
    print(f"Sealed exam accuracy: {exam_accuracy:.3f}")

    # --- The forward walk by hand, in code ---
    # (verifies our pencil result: x = [0.5, -1.2] should give ~ 0.622)
    def manual_forward_pass(X, weights):
        A = X.copy()
        for i in range(0, len(weights) - 2, 2):     # middle rooms: ReLU
            W, b = weights[i], weights[i+1]
            Z = A @ W + b
            A = np.maximum(0.0, Z)                   # zero-out squasher
        W, b = weights[-2], weights[-1]              # final room: Sigmoid
        Z = A @ W + b
        Z = np.clip(Z, -80, 80)                      # clip before Sigmoid to prevent gear shatter
        A = 1.0 / (1.0 + np.exp(-Z))
        return A

  !! WARN: SCALER.FIT ON TRAIN ONLY
     scaler.fit_transform(X_train)  -- RIGHT: mean/spread from study pile only
     scaler.fit_transform(X)        -- WRONG: exam pile leaks into the ruler
     Never call fit or fit_transform on X_val, X_test, or the full X.

  !! WARN: NP.CLIP BEFORE NP.EXP
     np.exp(-Z) for Z = -500 returns inf (gear shatter).
     np.clip(Z, -80, 80) before np.exp(-Z) prevents this.
     The clip changes the output by less than 10^(-34). Always clip.
