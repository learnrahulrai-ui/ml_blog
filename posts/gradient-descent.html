Gradient Descent: SGD, Adam, and Why Your Loss Explodes
Posted: 2024-09-12  |  Author: Rahul Rai  |  Tags: optimization, training

Gradient descent is the engine of all neural network training. Pick the right variant and your model converges in hours. Pick wrong (or forget to tune your learning rate) and you're watching NaN losses at 3 AM.

Vanilla Gradient Descent

The update rule is simple: move each parameter in the direction that decreases the loss.

w ← w − η · ∇L(w)

Where η (eta) is the learning rate. Too big → overshoot, loss diverges. Too small → training takes forever. 

# Vanilla GD (batch gradient descent)
for epoch in range(n_epochs):
    loss, grads = compute_loss_and_grads(X, y, params)
    for p, g in zip(params, grads):
        p -= lr * g

The problem: you compute gradients on the entire dataset before each update. For a million samples, that's one update per million forward passes. Slow.

Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD)

Update after every single sample (or a mini-batch of ~32–256 samples). Noisy, but fast. 

# Mini-batch SGD
for epoch in range(n_epochs):
    for X_batch, y_batch in dataloader:       # iterate mini-batches
        loss, grads = compute_loss_and_grads(X_batch, y_batch, params)
        for p, g in zip(params, grads):
            p -= lr * g

The noise from mini-batches can actually help: it prevents the model from getting stuck in sharp local minima. Modern research suggests large batches often generalize worse than small ones for this reason.

SGD + Momentum

Pure SGD oscillates. Momentum adds a velocity term: accumulate gradients over time so you don't reverse direction on every step.

v ← βv − η∇L     w ← w + v 
beta = 0.9
velocity = [np.zeros_like(p) for p in params]

for epoch in range(n_epochs):
    for X_batch, y_batch in dataloader:
        grads = compute_grads(X_batch, y_batch, params)
        for i, (p, g) in enumerate(zip(params, grads)):
            velocity[i] = beta * velocity[i] - lr * g
            p += velocity[i]

Adam: The One Everyone Uses

Adam (Adaptive Moment Estimation) combines momentum with per-parameter adaptive learning rates. Two running averages: first moment (mean of gradients) and second moment (mean of squared gradients).

m ← β₁m + (1−β₁)g    v ← β₂v + (1−β₂)g²    w ← w − η · m̂/(√v̂ + ε) 
def adam(params, grads, m, v, t,
         lr=1e-3, beta1=0.9, beta2=0.999, eps=1e-8):
    t += 1
    for i, (p, g) in enumerate(zip(params, grads)):
        m[i] = beta1 * m[i] + (1 - beta1) * g
        v[i] = beta2 * v[i] + (1 - beta2) * g**2

        # bias correction (important early in training)
        m_hat = m[i] / (1 - beta1**t)
        v_hat = v[i] / (1 - beta2**t)

        p -= lr * m_hat / (np.sqrt(v_hat) + eps)
    return t

Default hyperparameters (lr=1e-3, β₁=0.9, β₂=0.999) work well for most problems. This is why Adam is the standard starting point.

Why Does Your Loss Explode?

Common causes, in rough order of likelihood:

  • Learning rate too high. Try dividing it by 10. This fixes 80% of explosions.
  • No gradient clipping. For RNNs and Transformers, clip gradients: torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), max_norm=1.0)
  • Bad weight initialization. Use PyTorch defaults (Kaiming for ReLU networks, Xavier for tanh/sigmoid).
  • NaN in your data. Check inputs. assert not torch.isnan(X).any()
  • Loss function mismatch. Using BCELoss without sigmoid on your output? Use BCEWithLogitsLoss instead.

Learning Rate Schedules

The learning rate you start with shouldn't be the one you end with. Common schedules:

  • Cosine annealing — smooth decay, works well for LLMs
  • Linear warmup → cosine decay — the default for Transformers
  • ReduceLROnPlateau — drop lr when validation loss stalls
  • 1-cycle policy — fast.ai's trick: ramp up then anneal
# PyTorch cosine annealing with warmup (simplified)
from torch.optim.lr_scheduler import CosineAnnealingLR

optimizer = torch.optim.Adam(model.parameters(), lr=1e-3)
scheduler = CosineAnnealingLR(optimizer, T_max=n_epochs)

for epoch in range(n_epochs):
    train_one_epoch(model, optimizer)
    scheduler.step()

Quick Reference: Which Optimizer?

  • Just starting out: Adam, lr=1e-3
  • Training a Transformer / LLM: AdamW + cosine schedule + warmup
  • Fine-tuning a pretrained model: AdamW, lr=1e-4 to 5e-5
  • Research paper that needs to generalize: SGD + momentum (often beats Adam on final accuracy)
Adam gets you there faster. SGD sometimes gets you somewhere better. Train with Adam, optionally finish with SGD.
