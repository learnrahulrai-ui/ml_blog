What Machine Learning Actually Does (Before Anyone Tells You the Fancy Words)
Posted: 2026-06-03  |  Author: Rahul Rai  |  Tags: fundamentals, supervised-learning, knn, linear-regression

I learned to predict California house prices two different ways. One was invented in 1951. Neither required understanding a single buzzword first.

I started learning machine learning the way most people do — by Googling it. Within five minutes I had seventeen tabs open, each one drowning in words like hyperparameter, cross-validated RMSE, and gradient descent. Every explanation assumed I already knew the explanation.

So I closed the tabs and started over. From scratch. With a pencil.

What follows is what I actually learned — not the vocabulary, but the machinery. The ideas underneath the labels. If you understand this post, you understand the real thing. The fancy words are just stickers you can paste on later.

The Setup: A Table and a Question

Someone hands you a table. Each row is a neighbourhood in California. The columns record things you can measure — median income, average number of rooms, how old the houses are, how close the ocean is, how many people live there.

One column sits on the far right: the price.

   rooms  income  age  ocean  people  │  PRICE
   ──────────────────────────────────────────
    4.2    3.5    25    1.0    320    │   1.4
    5.1    5.6    10    0.5    210    │   2.8
    3.8    2.1    42    3.2    890    │   0.9
    ...                              │   ...

The question: given a new neighbourhood where you know everything except the price — can you guess it?

That's it. That is the entire premise of supervised learning. A table. A missing answer column. A guess.

⚠ GOTCHA — .shape is a PAIR When you measure the table with .shape, you get back (rows, cols) — a sealed package of two counts in a fixed order, not rows×cols. For this dataset: (20640, 9). That's 20,640 neighbourhoods, 9 things known about each.

The One Rule You Can't Break

Before you build anything, you hide part of the table.

Take 20% of the rows, fold them face-down, and push them to the side. You are not allowed to look at their prices. Not while building. Not while tuning. Not until the very end, when you open them once to see how you did. 

   ████████████████░░░░
    80% you study     20% sealed — don't touch

Why? Because if you study the answers and then "predict" those same answers, of course you'll look brilliant. You memorised the exam. That's not learning. The sealed 20% is your honesty check — the only way to know if your machine actually generalises to houses it has never seen.

★ THE KEY IDEA This split is the single most important idea in the field. Everything else is technique. This is discipline. Shuffle first — with a fixed seed, so the shuffle is identical every run — then cut.

Method 1: Ask the Neighbours

The first approach is embarrassingly simple. No equations. No dials to tune. Just one idea:

Things that are similar probably cost about the same.

To guess the price of an unknown neighbourhood, you measure how far it is from every neighbourhood you do know. "Distance" here means the combined gap across all the measurements — rooms, income, age, everything — squashed into a single number.

distance = √( gap&sub1;² + gap&sub2;² + gap&sub3;² + … )

Pick the 5 closest matches. Average their prices. That's your guess.

I did this on paper first, with a new neighbourhood at income 5.0:

   Known neighbours:
     Income 5.6 → Price 3.4    (distance 0.6)  ← close
     Income 4.0 → Price 2.7    (distance 1.0)  ← close
     Income 3.8 → Price 2.4    (distance 1.2)  ← close
     Income 2.5 → Price 1.5    (distance 2.5)
     Income 7.3 → Price 3.5    (distance 2.3)

   3 nearest: prices 3.4, 2.7, 2.4
   Guess = average = 2.83  →  roughly $283,000

That's the whole method. The computer does this across 16,000 rows instead of 5, but the logic is identical. This idea was first published in 1951 by Evelyn Fix and Joseph Hodges. It's older than most programming languages.

The number of neighbours you consult — k — is a dial you set before running. k=1 copies the single closest match (fast, but fragile). k=25 smooths out noise by averaging many (stable, but blurry). The right k is somewhere in between.

Measuring Mistakes

You need a number that says: how wrong were my guesses, overall?

   For each guess:     miss = guess − truth
   Kill the sign:      square it
   Across all guesses: take the mean
   Make it readable:   take the square root

   miss → square → mean → √  =  RMSE

RMSE stands for root mean squared error. If your RMSE is 0.77, your guesses are off by about $77,000 on average. Lower is better. Zero means you're psychic.

Method 2: Fit a Straight Line

Completely different philosophy. Instead of copying neighbours, you build a formula:

guess = income×dial&sub1; + rooms×dial&sub2; + age×dial&sub3; + … + nudge

Every measurement gets a dial — a multiplier that says how much that measurement matters. The nudge is a baseline, the starting point when every measurement is zero.

The best set of dials is whichever makes the total mistake smallest. Imagine a bowl — the mistake is the height, and you're looking for the bottom: 

         ╲         ╱
          ╲       ╱
           ╲     ╱
            ╲   ╱
             ╲ ╱
              •  ← smallest mistake = best dials

For a straight-line formula, there's a direct solution. You don't need to search — you can solve for the exact bottom using a system of equations. I worked it through on three points to convince myself it's real (nudge = 1/3, dial = 3/2). Fitting a straight line is geometrically a projection: the best line is the shadow of the data onto the space of possible lines.

Choosing Fairly: The Rotation Trick

You have a dial to pick (how many neighbours, or which formula complexity). You can't use the sealed 20% to choose — that's the honesty check, and you only open it once. So how do you pick the best setting? You rotate through the training data itself.

Cut the 80% study pile into 5 equal slices. Hold one slice out, train on the other four, score against the held-out slice. Rotate: 

            A    B    C    D    E
   round 1  ⬛   ·    ·    ·    ·    → score₁
   round 2  ·    ⬛   ·    ·    ·    → score₂
   round 3  ·    ·    ⬛   ·    ·    → score₃
   round 4  ·    ·    ·    ⬛   ·    → score₄
   round 5  ·    ·    ·    ·    ⬛   → score₅
                              average → honest practice score

Every row gets exactly one turn as the test. No row is always safe. The average of the five scores is a trustworthy estimate of how well that setting will perform on genuinely unseen data. Do this for every candidate setting. Pick the winner. Then — and only then — open the sealed test set. Run the winner once. Report the result.

▸ "Isn't scoring on the test set peeking?" No. The rule is: never let the test pick a setting. Using the test to choose among machines is cheating. Using it once to grade the locked winner is its entire job. If the test score is slightly worse than the practice score, that's normal — rehearsal always goes a little better than opening night. If it's much worse, the machine memorised instead of learned.

The Punchline

Two methods. One copies neighbours. One fits a formula. Both are trying to answer the same question: given what I've seen, what's my best guess for something I haven't?

  1. A table of numbers, one column is the answer.
  2. Hide some rows to stay honest.
  3. Guess the missing answers using the rows you kept.
  4. Measure how wrong you are: miss → square → mean → root.
  5. Choose your settings on a practice rotation, never on the real test.
  6. Open the sealed set exactly once. Report the truth.

That's supervised learning. Not the word — the thing.
