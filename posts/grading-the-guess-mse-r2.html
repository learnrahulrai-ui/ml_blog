Two Rulers for One Guess: MSE and R²
Posted: 2026-06-04  |  Author: Rahul Rai  |  Tags: evaluation, mse, r-squared, regression

Same straight-stick rule as before — a dial on each column, multiply, add, plus a fixed nudge. New sheet, though: cars now, not houses. And a new question that turns out to be the whole point: once the stick has drawn its line, was the line any good? Two different rulers answer that. They read the very same guesses and report two different verdicts.

Same Stick, New Sheet

   displacement  horsepower  weight  acceleration  │  MPG  ← right answer
   ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
      307          130        3504       12.0      │  18
      350          165        3693       11.5      │  15
       ...                                         │  ...
   one row = one car                               │  the answer
guess = d₁·disp + d₂·horse + d₃·weight + d₄·accel + nudge

Pick four columns, guess the fifth. The machinery is exactly the straight-stick rule — set the dials by shrinking the squared leftover, one dot-product per guess. Nothing new to build. The new work is grading.

Ruler One: MSE — Just the Size of the Miss

Earlier the mistake-score was RMSE. Four steps, in order:

   miss → square → mean → root   = RMSE
   miss → square → mean → STOP    = MSE

Stare at the name and it gives itself away:

   M S E
   │ │ └ Error    the miss   (y − guess)
   │ └── Squared  kill the sign
   └──── Mean     average them

No R. No Root. So do the first three steps and stop — leave off the ** 0.5 you tacked on last time.

MSE = (1/n) Σi (yi − ŷi)²
▸ WHAT MSE IS, EXACTLY MSE is RMSE squared, so it lives in squared answer-units — "miles per gallon, squared," which no one can picture. That is the price of dropping the root. What you buy: MSE is the exact thing the straight-stick rule shrinks when it sets the dials, so it is the rule's own home ruler. RMSE is just MSE pulled back into plain units for a human to read. Same ordering of which fit is better — the root is a rising step, so it never swaps the order.

The Problem MSE Can't Solve

   "the guess was off by 9."

   good?  bad?   ...compared to WHAT?

A raw miss-size has no scale of its own. Off by 9 mpg is dreadful for a car; off by 9 feet is wonderful for an archer firing across a valley. The number alone won't say. You need something to measure it against. That is the second ruler.

Ruler Two: R² — A Race Against a Fool

R² grades the stick against the dumbest honest guesser there is. So start with that fool. Forget the columns — here is a pile of past answers and no clues at all: 

   answers:  2   4   9   3   7

The fool must shout one number for everything. What is safest? The average, 5 — not because it is clever, but because it sits at the bottom of the squared-miss bowl. Any other single number misses more: 

   shout 5 → miss² = 9 + 1 + 16 + 4 + 4 = 34
   shout 6 → miss² = 16 + 4 + 9 + 9 + 1 = 39   (worse)
★ WHY THE AVERAGE IS THE FLOOR Shout some number c. Total squared miss is f(c) = Σ(yi − c)². Tip it to find the bottom: f′(c) = −2Σ(yi − c) = 0 gives c = (1/n)Σyi — the average, exactly. The curve is a bowl (second slope 2n > 0), so that point is the lowest, not the highest. The fool's best constant shout is always the mean.

Call the fool's total misery the total wobble. Now race:

   truth:          2     4     9     3     7

   FOOL  (says 5):  miss² =  9, 1, 16, 4, 4   → 34   ← total wobble
   STICK (uses cols): says 2.5, 4.5, 8, 3.5, 6.5
                      miss² = .25,.25,1,.25,.25 →  2   ← leftover
⚠ LEFTOVER IS THE BAD PART, NOT THE GOOD PART It is tempting to read "leftover = 2" as the stick's score. It is the opposite: leftover is the misery still left after the stick tried — the small bad bit it could not explain. The good part is what the stick ate: total wobble − leftover. 
   total wobble  ████████████████████   34   (the fool's whole job)
   stick ATE     ██████████████████     32   = 34 − 2   (the good part)
   leftover      █                       2   (the bad bit still left)

R² is the slice the stick ate, as a fraction of the whole bar:

R² = (total wobble − leftover) / total wobble = 32 / 34 ≈ 0.94
⚠ DIVIDE BY THE WHOLE BAR, NOT THE COUNT The divider is the whole wobble (34), not the row count (5). R² asks "what fraction of the whole wobble did you eat?" — a slice of a bar, not an average. The count sits on top and bottom and cancels, which is the very reason R² carries no units.

The scale falls right out:

   R² = 1.0   ate everything, leftover zero   — a perfect line
   R² = 0.0   ate nothing — no better than the fool
   R² < 0     WORSE than the fool — the line actively hurts

It can never top 1: you cannot eat more than the whole bar. But it can dip below 0, and on a sheet the stick has never seen, it sometimes does.

Guess Once, Grade Twice

   set the dials ─► guess the sealed pile ─► guesses (computed ONCE)
                                                  │
                                       ┌──────────┴──────────┐
                                       ▼                     ▼
                                 MSE → a SIZE           R² → a SLICE

The two rulers do not need two sets of guesses. You guess once, then hold both rulers against the same misses. MSE says how big the misses are; R² says how much better than a fool you did. 

from sklearn.metrics import mean_squared_error, r2_score

def evaluate_guesser(model, X_test, y_test):
    guesses = model.predict(X_test)              # guess ONCE
    mse = round(mean_squared_error(y_test, guesses), 3)   # ruler 1: a size
    r2  = round(r2_score(y_test, guesses), 3)             # ruler 2: a slice
    return (mse, r2)                              # truth first, guesses second — both rulers

And the old discipline holds: open the sealed pile once, to grade — never to choose. Grading on rows the stick never saw is the whole job of the hidden pile.

Three Things Worth Stating Precisely

▸ ON THE TRAINING PILE THE BAR SPLITS CLEANLY — ON THE TEST PILE IT NEED NOT With a nudge column, the straight-stick rule forces its leftovers to sit at right-angles to its guesses, so on the training pile the bar splits exactly: total wobble = ate + leftover (textbook: TSS = ESS + RSS), and R² lands in [0, 1]. On the test pile no such right-angle holds: the leftover can be larger than the wobble, so R² = 1 − RSS/TSS can fall below zero. A negative test R² is not a bug — it is the line doing worse than shouting the average.
▸ ONE COLUMN: R² IS JUST CORRELATION, SQUARED Fit a straight stick on a single column and R² equals the squared correlation between that column and the answer — that is literally why it wears the name "R squared" (r is the correlation). With several columns it generalises to the squared correlation between the answers and the stick's guesses.
⚠ R² ALWAYS RISES WHEN YOU ADD A COLUMN — ON THE TRAINING PILE Hand the stick one more column and training R² never drops, even if the column is pure noise — the old fit is still available, so it can only do at least as well. That is why training R² is a poor judge of whether a column earns its place: use the test R², or the adjusted R² that docks you for each extra dial.
★ WHERE THE MISS-SIZE COMES FROM (BIAS vs VARIANCE) Average a guesser's test MSE over many possible training piles and it splits three ways: E[MSE] = bias² + variance + noise. Bias is the line being too stiff to follow the true shape; variance is the line being too jumpy, swinging with each pile it sees; noise is the part no rule can ever remove. The stiff-vs-jumpy dial from the neighbours post is exactly the bias-vs-variance trade, seen through MSE.
  1. MSE = miss → square → mean → stop. It is RMSE without the root, in squared answer-units, and it is the very thing the stick shrinks.
  2. A raw miss-size means nothing alone — "off by 9" needs something to measure against.
  3. R² races the stick against a fool who only ever shouts the average (the bottom of the squared-miss bowl).
  4. R² = (total wobble − leftover) / total wobble = 1 − RSS/TSS. No units. Tops out at 1; can go below 0 on unseen rows.
  5. Guess once, grade twice: the same guesses feed both rulers.
